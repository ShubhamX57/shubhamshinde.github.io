I grew up in Pune, India, trained first as a mechanical engineer, and somewhere in the middle of simulating wind turbine blades in CFD, I realised the most interesting problems were the computational ones. That instinct eventually led me to a PG Diploma in Data Science & AI, award-winning research in aerial image analysis, and now an MSc at Bristol funded by the SARTHI National Scholarship.

Three years of industry work — from a aero research laboratory to an ML startup to clinical imaging — have given me a healthy respect for what production software actually demands: thorough testing, reproducible environments, and systems that someone else can understand at 2 am when something breaks.

My current research is in quasi-elastic neutron scattering (QENS) at Bristol, building open-source analysis tools aligned with workflows at facilities like ISIS Neutron Source. The physics is fascinating — watching diffusion models live or die against experimental data — and the engineering challenge of making that rigorous, fast, and shareable is exactly the kind of problem I want to keep solving.

Outside of work I am the student academic representative for our MSc cohort, I manage digital platforms for two professional engineering societies, and I remain quietly convinced that a good README is an act of kindness.

§ 3 — Scientific & software projects

QENS Analysis & Visualisation Suite
MSc Capstone · University of Bristol · Aligned with ISIS Neutron Source workflows
2025 – present

Open-source pipeline for quasi-elastic neutron scattering (QENS) analysis — my MSc capstone at Bristol. The project implements the full physics chain from raw HDF5 experimental files through resolution correction, model fitting, and Bayesian parameter estimation, presenting results through a FastAPI + React web interface.

The core physical model is the Chudley-Elliott jump-diffusion framework, which describes how molecules hop between sites in a liquid or confined geometry. Getting the resolution function deconvolution right was the decisive technical challenge — a naive approach inflates the diffusion coefficient by ~20%.

Chudley-Elliott structure factor (QENS model)
$$S(Q,\omega) = \frac{1}{\pi} \cdot \frac{\Gamma(Q)}{\omega^2 + \Gamma^2(Q)}$$ where $\Gamma(Q) = \frac{D\,Q^2}{1 + Q^2 l^2}$, $D$ = diffusion coefficient, $l$ = jump length
Resolution-corrected measured spectrum
$$I(Q,\omega) = S(Q,\omega) \otimes R(Q,\omega) + B(Q)$$ $R(Q,\omega)$ = instrumental resolution function, $B(Q)$ = background
Open Source Repository
github.com/ShubhamX57/QENS
Key scientific achievements
  • 16% reduction in systematic error in the diffusion coefficient via physics-correct resolution correction
  • 3× improvement in χ² goodness-of-fit: Chudley-Elliott vs Fickian diffusion model
  • Bayesian MCMC across 4 independent chains with Gelman-Rubin $\hat{R} < 1.01$ verified convergence
  • FastAPI + React web interface in progress with cloud deployment
The 3× χ² improvement over Fickian is the result I'm proudest of — it means the physics model actually matters, not just the software.
